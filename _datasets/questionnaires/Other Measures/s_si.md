#### Detailed description

The Superiority Illusion (SI) measure assesses the "better-than-average effect," a well-documented cognitive bias in which individuals overestimate their own abilities and characteristics relative to others. This illusion of superiority represents a form of positive self-enhancement that is common across cultures and domains.

The measure typically involves presenting participants with a series of personality trait words or ability descriptors and asking them to rate how these traits describe them compared to an average peer or the general population.

**Administration**: Participants rate themselves on various traits using a scale (e.g., sliding scale or Likert-type rating) indicating whether they believe they possess more, less, or the same amount of each trait compared to the average person.

**Theoretical Background**: The superiority illusion has been linked to:
- Self-serving bias in attribution
- Metacognitive monitoring abilities
- Dopaminergic activity in resting-state brain networks
- Individual differences in optimism and self-esteem

#### Scoring

**Mean Superiority Score**: Typically calculated as the average across all trait ratings, with higher scores indicating a stronger tendency to view oneself as superior to others.

The superiority illusion shows individual differences and has been found to correlate with:
- Resting-state brain network connectivity
- Dopamine D1 receptor availability
- Metacognitive performance
- Optimism and positive affect
