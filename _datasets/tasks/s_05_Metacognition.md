#### Detailed description

##### Visual discrimination task
The stimulus is a Gabor patch submerged in white noise. The Gabor is tilted either to the left or to the right. Stimulus size is 3 degrees of visual angle with 2 cycles per degree. Signal-to-noise ratio between the Gabor and white noise is controlled by the 3-down-1-up staircase throughout the task. On each trial, a light grey fixation cross is first presented in the center of a black screen for a random interval between 600 and 1000 ms. Then the target stimulus is presented for 16.7 ms. The target is followed again by the fixation cross display, and the participant has to respond whether the Gabor was tilted to the left or to the right. After every trial, participants rated their confidence regarding the correctness of a given answer on a 6-point scale (50-100%). 
##### Auditory discrimination task
The stimulus is a short chord submerged in a longer snippet of white noise. The target chord has either a high or a low pitch. Participants are familiarized with both chords during the introduction and practice trials. Signal-to-noise ratio between the chord amplitude and white noise amplitude is controlled by the 3-down-1-up staircase throughout the task. On each trial a light grey fixation cross is presented in the center of a black screen and remains there until the first response of the participant. At the same time, white noise starts playing. At a random interval between 600 and 1000 ms, the target chord is presented for 33 ms. The total duration of the noise is always 1.2 seconds. The participants had to respond whether the target chord was high or low and then rate their confidence in a manner identical to the visual task (see above).
##### Visual & auditory detection tasks (only in Krakow)
The stimuli in these tasks are the same as for the visual and auditory discrimination tasks, except that on half of the trials, there is only noise and no target signal. Participants have to decide whether there was any target in the noise or not (yes/no answer), and rate their confidence in this decision on the six-point confidence scale (50-100%).
